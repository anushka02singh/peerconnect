The Problem

Students are drowning in too many tabs

PeerConnect started as an idea during an undergraduate HCI class — a simple question: why do students need five different apps just to get through the week? Canvas for assignments, Google Calendar for scheduling, GroupMe for coordinating, Quizlet for studying, email for announcements. None of them talk to each other, and none of them were designed with the student experience as the primary concern.

In the final weeks of this HCD course, I took that original idea and turned it into a full high-fidelity prototype in Figma — following the human-centered design process from research through to an interactive, clickable product. The core question became: how do you give students one place to see what's due and immediately start working on it, without any friction in between?

"How might we design a mobile app that helps Rutgers students stay on top of their academic workload and actively prepare for it — all in one place, without bouncing between five different tools?"

Design Statement

Market Research

What's already out there — and what's missing

Before designing anything, I looked at the tools students currently use to understand where the gaps are. The pattern was clear: every existing tool either does too little or exists in a completely separate silo.

Tool What it does Why it's relevant What's missing
Canvas LMS Assignments, grades, announcements Same audience; academic organization No peer connection Poor mobile UX
Rutgers App Maps, bus, dining, basic course info Same student audience, all-in-one concept No study tools No Canvas sync
Quizlet Flashcards, practice tests Study tools overlap; same student users No scheduling Completely siloed
GroupMe / Discord Group messaging, community Peer communication; study coordination No academic structure No Canvas sync
Google Calendar Scheduling, reminders Overlaps with deadline tracking Not academic-specific Students use it separately

The main takeaway: no single tool combines academic organization with study resources. Students have to stitch these together themselves, and that friction is the design problem PeerConnect is trying to solve.

Focused Scope

Narrowing down to what actually matters

The original vision for PeerConnect was ambitious — peer matching, social features, notifications, Canvas integration, and more. Based on feedback, I narrowed the final project scope to two features that form a complete, meaningful user journey on their own:

Design Focus

A smart home dashboard that shows students exactly what's due and when — connected directly to a study hub where they can take action on it.

01

Home Dashboard

The command center. Shows upcoming deadlines pulled from Canvas, a daily and weekly calendar view, and smart nudges based on what's most urgent. The core design challenge: how do you surface the most important thing without overwhelming the student?

Information Hierarchy
02

Study Hub

The action layer. Flashcards, practice tests, a collaborative guide editor, and a quiz game mode — all tied to specific courses. The core design challenge: how do you make studying feel less like a chore inside the same app you use to check deadlines?

Engagement & Motivation

Everything else — peer matching, friends, notifications — gets scoped to future work. This isn't shrinking the vision, it's being intentional about what gets designed well.

Design Process

Following the HCD process start to finish

This project follows the human-centered design process — from initial research through to a polished interactive prototype. The idea originated in a previous HCI class; the design work happened over the final weeks of this HCD course.

1
Weeks 1–2

Market Research

Analyzed five competing tools — Canvas, Rutgers App, Quizlet, GroupMe, and Google Calendar — to map where existing solutions fall short and identify the gap PeerConnect fills.

2
Weeks 3–4

User Research

Survey of Rutgers students focusing on current tool usage, pain points around deadline management, and how they currently study. Key finding: students want one place for everything, not another app to switch between.

3
Week 5

Synthesize & Define

Organized research findings into themes, created two personas, and mapped a student journey from "checking what's due" to "actually studying for it" — revealing the gap between knowing and doing.

4
Weeks 6–10

Prototype

Built a high-fidelity interactive prototype in Figma covering the full core experience — onboarding, home dashboard (Today, This Week, and Canvas views), and the complete Study Hub flow including flashcards, practice tests, and collaborative guide.

5
Weeks 11–14

Test & Iterate

Usability testing with Rutgers students, focusing on whether the dashboard hierarchy made urgency clear and whether students could navigate between deadline awareness and active studying without friction.

Key Design Decisions

What shaped the final design

⚡

Urgency without anxiety

Deadline cards use color-coded urgency bars (red, amber, green) alongside text labels — never color alone. Students can scan the dashboard in under three seconds and know what needs attention.

🔗

Dashboard → Study in one tap

The home screen nudge card links directly to an active study session. The deadline list links directly into the Study Hub for the relevant course. The whole point is zero friction between knowing and doing.

📚

Study tools that feel light

Flashcards show a mastery progress strip at the top so students can see how far they've come, not just how far they have to go. Small detail — big difference in motivation.

🤝

Collaboration built in

The collaborative study guide isn't a separate feature — it's the first thing you see in the Study Hub. Real-time contributor avatars and an inline comment thread make it feel live, not static.

🎓

Canvas as infrastructure

Rather than replacing Canvas, PeerConnect treats it as infrastructure. Deadlines, grades, and course data all sync in — the student never has to re-enter information they already put in Canvas.

🌙

Dark theme by default

Students use apps late at night before exams. The dark navy theme isn't just aesthetic — it's a practical decision that reduces eye strain during the exact moments the app gets used most.

What's Next

Where PeerConnect goes from here

The two core features — home dashboard and study hub — form a complete, usable product on their own. But the original vision had more, and the foundation is designed to support it. Future work would focus on:

🔍 Peer Matching by Course
👥 Friends & Social Layer
🔔 Unified Notifications
📊 Grade Tracking Dashboard
🎮 Multiplayer Quiz Mode
♿ Accessibility Audit (WCAG AA)
📱 Android Version
🧪 Usability Testing Round 2

The most important next step is usability testing with real Rutgers students — not to validate the design, but to find where it breaks down. The most valuable design insights come from watching someone use something you built and seeing exactly where they get stuck.

That's the whole point of human-centered design. You're never really done.
